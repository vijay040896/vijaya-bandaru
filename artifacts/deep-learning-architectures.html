Understanding Deep Learning Architectures: From Theory to Ethical Practice

Introduction

This artifact comes from a class module on deep learning architectures, where I worked through how Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs), Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs), and Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) actually learn, and grappled with the ethical implications of generative AI, including synthetic media like deepfakes. Where my first two artifacts show hands-on building and comparative algorithm analysis, this one shows a different skill: theoretical grounding and ethical reasoning about the technology I'm building with.

Description

Rather than reading the architectures as a static list of definitions, I used a dialogue with Claude to actively work through each one: what problem it's built to solve, how it learns, and where it breaks down. That included the role activation functions play in letting a network learn anything beyond a straight line, and a direct look at how GANs' generator/discriminator setup, the same mechanism used for useful synthetic data, is also what makes deepfakes possible.

Work Sample — Excerpt from the Learning Dialogue:

Me: "Why can't a plain ANN handle images well, and what does a CNN actually do differently?"

Claude: "A plain ANN connects every input pixel to every neuron in the next layer, so a modest image produces an enormous number of parameters, and the network has no sense that nearby pixels are related. A CNN instead slides small filters across the image, so it learns local patterns, edges, textures, shapes, that apply no matter where they appear in the frame, and stacks those patterns into more complex features layer by layer."

Me: "And a GAN is doing something completely different from both of those?"

Claude: "Right, a GAN pits two networks against each other, a generator trying to produce convincing fake data and a discriminator trying to catch the fakes, and they improve together through that competition. It's the same mechanism behind both useful synthetic training data and deepfakes; the architecture itself is ethically neutral, the application is what raises the concern."

Objective

My goal was to build a working conceptual model of each architecture, specific enough that I can reason about which one fits a given real-world problem, rather than just recognizing the acronyms, and to think seriously about the ethical responsibility that comes with generative techniques before I ever deploy them.

Process

Working Through Each Architecture

I went architecture by architecture, ANNs as the baseline, CNNs for spatial/image data, RNNs for sequential data like text or time series, and GANs for generation, asking Claude to explain not just the mechanism but why that mechanism is the right fit for its typical use case, and where activation functions fit into letting any of them learn non-linear patterns at all.

Connecting Theory to Real Scenarios

For each architecture, I pushed the dialogue toward concrete, real-world applications (image recognition for CNNs, language and forecasting for RNNs, synthetic data generation for GANs) so the theory stayed tied to problems I might actually encounter building AI agents, rather than staying abstract.

Reasoning Through the Ethics

I specifically worked through the ethical implications of generative techniques, deepfakes in particular, discussing where the line falls between legitimate synthetic-data use (augmenting training sets, privacy-preserving data sharing) and harmful misuse (impersonation, misinformation), and what responsibilities that puts on someone building these systems.

Connecting to Change Leadership

Given my background leading enterprise system rollouts, I also connected this material to change leadership in AI/ML integration: understanding these architectures well enough to set realistic expectations, and understanding their ethical risk profile well enough to build in the right safeguards, are both things a leader introducing AI into an organization needs to own, not delegate away entirely to the technical team.

Tools and Technologies Used

Claude (Anthropic) as an AI dialogue partner to explain, question, and stress-test my understanding of ANN, CNN, RNN, and GAN architectures.

Value Proposition

Unique Value

This artifact rounds out my portfolio with a skill the first two don't show on their own: theoretical depth and ethical judgment, not just the ability to build an AI tool or compare algorithms, but the ability to explain why a given architecture works, when it doesn't apply, and what risks come with it.

Relevance

Combined with my Appian background and my goal of building AI agents professionally, this matters because leading AI/ML integration in an enterprise setting requires more than implementation skill; it requires being able to explain architectural tradeoffs to non-technical stakeholders and to responsibly navigate the ethical questions generative AI raises, both of which this artifact demonstrates directly.
